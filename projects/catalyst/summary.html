Catalyst

Catalyst was a game that was created as part of my Major Qualifying Project (MQP) at WPI, titled "Catalyst - Using Hitbox Scaling for Latency Compensation in a Cloud Based Game." The goal of the project was to create and implement a latency compensation technique in a custom game, which was to be deployed on Google Stadia. The idea behind this technique was to maintain player accuracy and quality of experience in spite of a player's latency. The project itself was sponsored by Google, who provided feedback on our work and the ability to deploy our game to Google Stadia for testing.

The team that I was a part of decided to create a multiplayer first person shooter called Catalyst. The idea was to have players cast spells at each other, rather than using guns like in many other FPS titles. Player can combine three different elements for a total of 10 unique spells in a Capture-the-Flag multiplayer arena.

Since I have always been a big fan of multiplayer first person shooters like Halo, Destiny, etc., I decided to work on the networking aspects of the game in order to learn more about how multiplayer games are structured and how I, as a game programmer, and create them. I started by learning about how to make a basic multiplayer game in Unreal Engine 4, which taught me the fundamentals of multiplayer works in the engine, such as the peer-to-peer architecture, how to create a lobby, connect other players through Steam matchmaking, and placing players into a match once everyone was ready. From there, I took that knowledge and worked on the other networking aspects of the game as well, such as updating match state across all player (like when a flag was captured) and adding features like being able to choose your team in the lobby. I created AI bots that would act as teammates for players, with behaviors for going after the other team's flag, bringing it home once they grabbed it, protecting allies who are carrying an enemy flag, and attacking enemies who are stealing their flag. Additionally, I created the main menu and helped my teammates debug any multiplayer-related issues.

Since no one on the team was a game artist, we used previously created assets from the Unreal marketplace, many of which came from the game Paragon, which was created by Epic Games but after cancellation released the assets for free online.

The game went through multiple stages of development. We first created a small demo, which was a single player game in which the player would throw fireballs at a moving NPC target. We gave this demo to a another group of student researchers, who ran a user study to determine how much hitbox scaling should be supplied in response to player latency to achieve a desired accuracy. We also brought the game to WPI's alphafest, where other students were able to playtest the game and provide feedback. Finally, we ran our own user study with the final version of the game on Google Stadia, where we measured player accuracy in response to artificially induced latency with hitbox scaling on and off. These results were written in a final report and presentation that we gave to Google.
