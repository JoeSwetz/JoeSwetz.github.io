Lift-Off Laetus

One of my final game development projects at Worcester Polytechnic Institute was for a class called Technical Game Development II. The class was taught alongside an advanced game art class, where the professors put students from each class into development teams, each with about 2-3 artists and 2-3 programmers. The group I was part of ended up created Lift-Off Laetus!

Lift-Off Laetus is a turned-based strategy game that takes place on the planet Laetus. Two teams of space-farers have crash landed on the planet and must fight for limited resources to fix their own ship and make it home. Both players control a team of three crew members. The game takes place on a grid of squares, and on each turn the player can move their crew around the board, attacking other players and collecting resources for their ship. The first team to collect three crates wins!

Development of the game was chronicled on our development team website, which you can find here. You'll find links to download the alpha and beta versions of the game, our final class presentation and our dev blog with weekly updates from the art and tech teams, giving insight into the development proess alongside screenshots of our work.

I personally worked on the programming side of things for this game with 3 other members on the tech team. The game was developed in Unreal Engine 4 using C++. One of the biggest parts of the game that I developed was the underlying mechanism for spawning the grid of squares on top of the terrain developed by the art team. I wanted to give us as much flexiblity as possible with regards to how we could set up the board, since things were likely to change quickly over the 7 week development period. On startup, the game reads the topology of the board from a config file and spawns the grid tiles on top of the terrain (so if the art team changes the landscape, the tiles would adjust automatically). The config file also specifies the location of obstacles, resources boxes, powerups, and holes in the grid. From an object-oriented perspective, this also made it simple to set up individual objects for each tile, which alloed to trach properties such the player standing on that tile and whether an effect had been applied to that tile by a crew member's ability.

I also worked on the input system for the game. Since we were develpoing a local multiplayer game during a period of remote learning during the Covid-19 pandemic, we had the challenge of figuring out how to test the game when no one could be in the same room. To get our alpha up and running, we had one player used the keyboard, while the other had to plug in a controller. During the alpha testing this became a pain point for some players (all testers had to play both sides on their own since we were all at home), especially those who did not have a controller to plug in. Therefore, in the final version of the game I created a more flexible input system that allowed the players to either use a single keyword (with one player using WASD and the other using arrow keys), or to plug in a controller for the second player. The player can choose their preferred input option from the main menu.

Additionally, I also worked on intergrating the art team's assets into the game. I was already very experienced with Unreal Engine 4 at the time due to my work on Catalyst and 33 Alder Ridge, so I was able to help the art team import their, where I could then attach them to characters, and carrying out animations in the game's C++ code.

Finally, I helped polish the game by fixing bugs, setting up a main menu, and creating a victory cinematic when a teams wins the game.
